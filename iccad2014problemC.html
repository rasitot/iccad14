Design for Manufacturability Flow for Advanced Semiconductor Nodes

Rasit O. Topaloglu, Ph.D.
IBM Corporation
(rasit at us dot ibm dot com)

Introduction

Design for manufacturability (DFM) is the science of characterization, modeling, and optimization of yield in semiconductor integrated circuits (ICs). It is a newer field as compared to more established VLSI design automation areas such as logic synthesis or place&route.; Due to this, not all design models or flows are optimal yet. Due also to more stringent manufacturing challenges, criteria that has not been important in the past may start becoming important at newer technology nodes.

As circuit optimization requires simultaneous considerations of several circuit aspects, improving yield may end up with race conditions against criteria such as performance, runtime, or layout size. All of these are important in semiconductor IC manufacturing and should be accounted for in a DFM flow.

In the proposed problem, we tackle a traditional DFM problem, albeit with new and important metrics. In particular, we target fill insertion in VLSI while considering criteria which in the past have not been too relevant.

Background

When a circuit is designed through either manual or automated placement and route of polygons that define devices, interconnects, or other passive devices, densities of these polygons within windows of interest may have a large variation across such windows in the rest of the layout. To balance the density of polygons, fills are inserted to bring the density of several windows to a uniform number which is picked by technology developers to be optimal.

As an input, your team will receive layouts with regions identified for which fills can be inserted. You will be provided basic design rules that you have to adhere to. (Violation of these rules will result in your submission to fail.)

The output will be a layout with fills that have been optimally inserted into the regions provided. (Insertion of fills outside the regions that are identified will result in your submission to fail. Abutting these regions is acceptable.)

To enable a fair comparison, usage of GPU will not be permitted. However, multicore processors and systems that contain such processors will be permitted. Wall-clock runtime will be used in scoring.

Input:

  • layout in GDS format with regions identified to fill: testcase s testcase b testcase m

  • density and average linewidth statistics for no-fill given as ASCII data files: s_M1 s_M2 s_M3 b_M1 b_M2 b_M3 m_M1 m_M2 m_M3. First two columns indicate window location, third column is the no-fill density within that window, and the last column shows the linewidth average in micrometers. These statistics from no-fill option needs to be used towards your solution, however the non-fillable areas in the testcases can be used to estimate these statistics until these files are provided.

  • design rules relevant to the problem: minimum metal width and space are 32nm, minimum metal area is 4800nm^2.

Output:

  • layout in GDS format with fills that adhere to provided design rules

Objectives:

  • minimize performance degradation (total area of fill and overlap of fill with neighboring (top and bottom if exists) layer non-fill regions). Accounts for 20% of total score.

  • minimize variation , i.e., chip density standard deviation. Deviations are added up accross layers. Accounts for 20% of total score.

  • minimize line deviation , i.e., density variations per window from column-based average - all for each layer separately. Variations are added up for score. Accounts for 20% of total score.

  • minimize outliers , i.e., density variations per window from plus or minus 3 sigma from chip layer mean. Variations are added up for score. Accounts for 20% of total score.

  • minimize runtime. Accounts for 15% of total score.

  • minimize layout size (final GDS size). Accounts for 5% of total score.

Evaluation:

  • The score will be computed based on the submitted solutions. Results with correct solutions will be considered for each portion of the score. Scores from individual portions will be added to constitute the final score. Final score is weighted average of all testcase scores. Success in select individual criterion will also be recognized across teams however submitted layouts must be free of GDS or design rule errors to win the contest.

Q&A;:

  1. What is the definition of each layer and what is the relation among layers?

    Layer 1 is at the very bottom. They are ordered by the layer numbers.

  2. What is the constraint of fill which would be inserted to increase density, like width, height, or shape?

    Fill and metal both use the same rules. You can only use rectangular fill.

  3. Can we use tool to help reading GDS files? or only use c++ code to read?

    Yes, any tool, library, etc. can be used.

  4. We were wondering how may you estimate the window density? we have seen some papers that have different computation is there any unified formula? And how do you evaluate "minimize variation"?

    We will use 20um non-overlapping square windows starting from the lower left corner.

  5. When I read the GDS file, I know not how to find the regions identified to fill. How do I recognize the regions?

    Any area that is covered can be filled. Empty regions cannot be filled.

  6. Can you explain it in detail? Because I think the empty regions should be fill and the covered area can not be filled (no place to fill). Moreover, the problem C said:" however the non-fillable areas in the testcases can be used to estimate these statistics for now." So I think the non-fillable area can not be empty regions.

    The GDS files uploaded shows where you can fill; they don't show what original features were. For the latter, only window by window statistics will be provided.

  7. We calculate the density of fillable region (read from s.gds) at window(0,0) at layer 10003, it is 0.953734 (381493600 nm2 / 400000000 nm2). And the no-fill density you gave us (in sM3.txt) in that window is 0.3288304. We are wondering why is that the fillable region area plus no-fill region area will exceed total window area.

    .txt files for the s testcase seem incorrect; I will update them.

  8. What is the definition of "linewidth" ? Can you give us an example?

    Smaller width of a rectangle.

  9. If we don't have the total number of "lines", how could we calculate the "linewidth average" after we insert dummy fills?

    You cannot, but you can find a way to estimate it.

  10. Are the fillable region (read from GDS file) and the original layout already adhere to the design rules? (Meaning the boundary of fillable region can fill)

    Fills can abut the fillable region boundary.

  11. Do the polygons in input files (s.gds/b.gds) stand for fillable region or original polygons and dummy fills ?

    Fillable region.

  12. about the first objectives it says "overlap of fill with neighboring (top and bottom if exists) layer non-fill regions", Does non-fill region mean the original blank region or it include the dummy which we had filled?

    You should also avoid the fills you inserted.

  13. Do our program read two files that one is GDS file and the other is density information file ?

    Yes.

  14. Our output gds file should be the no-fill region just like the input gds files, or should be the fill region we insert?

    Provide the fill you insert.

  15. About the final upload files, should we only upload the binary executive file of our code? What if some tools and libraries are used?

    Upload everything needed to run your code on the server.

  16. Can we use rectilinear polygons to fill? or only rectangle? If we can, how do we estimate linewidth?

    Only rectangles.

  17. Would you please do me the favor to tell me whether we can use some LP solvers to solve problem C, like cplex? Is it available?

    Only open source solvers can be used.

  18. About 3rd problem, we have question about linewidth. Based on Q&A;(9) we are told that we cannot c alculate linewidth in exact value. However, practically this is no way an issue for a layout. That is, average linewidth is easy to obtain from original layout. So, may you tell us what are your considerations to not giving the exact information? Furthermore, linewidth accounts for 30% total score, isn't it too much if we can only estimate it by taking chance, and by no means can we know whether our estimation is close?

    We will either provide more information about the layout, provide an estimation method, or remove this metric going forward.

  19. We are confused about one constraint: minimum metal width and space are 32nm, minimum metal area is 4800nm^2. Are all fills should follow this constraint, too? Can the constraint be considered as " minimum fill width and space are 32nm, minimum fill area is 4800nm^2." ? Or fills are not necessary to follow this constraint.

    Yes, fills also have to follow the same rules; fills are metal, too. Your example is correct.

  20. I have opened my GDS output in a layout viewer,but I did not find any DRC violations. Would you please tell me which DRC violation do we have?

    There was a mistake in my DRC script that caught the min area rectangles by mistake. You are indeed DRC clean.

  21. What does "db precision error" mean?

    Here is the exact error message; perhaps you can figure a solution if you recognize this value: ERROR: Rule file precision 1000 is not consistent with database precision 999.999952503 in input file DB is database. There is a default minimum dimension defined in each layout. Somehow when you have passed your layout features through various operations, this value has changed. Perhaps you can try to open your text layout on a very small example and see if the input and output ASCII layouts have a different parameter. You may be able to find such definition at the very top or bottom of your layout ASCII file. You may want to consider using a different GDS translator, too.

  22. As the release of the result of alpha test, will there be any explicit cost metric given? For example, there are 4 objectives for variation - std, diff, dev from chip mean and dev from row-based. Will they be weighted or just added up? And is the final score "a*performance+b*variation+c*runtime+d*size"? If so, will the weight be given?

    Unless the ratios specified in the website now, everything will have equal weight. This means every factor gets normalized first.

  23. In Objective 2, we need to minimize variation,which is consist of many items. Can you tell us how do you calculate the variation into that single fraction in alpha test results?

    For now, it was only the standard deviation of density.

  24. For each layer metal, is there a fixed type for dummy metals to choose? For example, there are three type (i.e. (width,length):(2,3)、(3,4)、(4,5))dummy metal pool to insert in layer 2, so I only can choose these three type to insert. If there isn't any constraint for dummy metal, we consider that we can insert any width or length of metal into fillable reigon. From problem description, we only know the lower bound for metal width is 32nm. We wonder that the dummy metal width should be fixed for each layer, and the length of dummy metals can be different. The length decides the final area of inserted dummy metal.

    Width need not be fixed. Any aspect ratio is OK.

  25. Is there any fixed placement way for dummy metal in each layer? For example, any inserted dummy metal in layer 1 only can be placed by horizontal or vertical direction?

    No such rule in this contest.

  26. We still confused the description, "overlap of fill with neighboring (top and bottom if exists) layer non-fill regions." and the answer of Q&A; #12. We wonder that the more overlaps happened, the worse the performance is. And what is the overlap definition? From the answer of Q&A; #12, you mean we should insert the dummy metal which won't overlap the fillable reigon which is on top or bottom layer?

    Your tool should minimize overlap of fills with non-filleable region and other fills you insert in neighboring layers.

  27. Are input’s GDS files only testcase s and testcase b provided on the website during Final Evaluation?

    There may be one ore more secret testcases.

  28. About Alpha Test Results of this time, what is the unit of runtime and size?

    Seconds and Bytes respectively.

  29. Q: Dose the objective"minimize the linewidth average difference" mean against the whole layout or each window?

    We have removed the linewidth optimization from the objectives.

  30. Q: How to calculate line deviation? I don’t know what column-based average is.

    The column of windows within which you are computing the deviation of a given window. The column of windows is a vertical slab of layout area where the width is defined by the window for which you are computing this metric.

  31. Q: In the latest revision, if we use parallel programming, how would runtime be calculated? second * #CPU or just overall runtime?

    We are working on a method to account for number of cores and maximum memory utilized. We will penalize these if we implement the monitoring system on time. Otherwise, we will go by runtime only. Number of cores would likely have a log dependency, i.e., runtime would be divided by log(#cores).

  32. Q: How did you calculate "performance degrade" based on total area and overlap area?

    We merge your fill results with original layout. Then take area overlaps between layers (1,2) and (2,3). We add the overlap area. There is no way you can compute the exact output as you do not have access to original layouts.

  33. Q: How did you calculate both "line hotspot" and "outlier hotspot"? Is "hotspot" a statistically well defined word? If so, sorry for our inadequate understanding, yet may you please define them explicitly?

    Hotspot is a layout area (window) selected based on a metric. The selection is made if the metric is beyond a certain threshold. A hotspot may have a score associated with it; such score may indicate severity with respect to the threshold.

  34. Q: Do you calculate the variation by this formula: sqr_root( sum( (window.density - chip mean)^2 ) / window_num)? Because our own calculation is quite different from the result released.

    It is "chip layer mean." Each layer is added at the end.

  35. Q: By Q&A;(32), do you mean that the "performance degrade" is only taking overlaps between (1,2) and (2,3) into consideration? How about total fill area?

    Beta submission only checked the overlap but final submission will also check area. Area will impact 5% and overlap will be 15% of total score.

  36. Q: Do the ASCII data files M1, M2, and M3 correspond to the lowest layer to highest layer (10001 -> 10003) or correspond to the descriptively ordered layer (10002/10001/10003) in gds file?

    Lowest to highest.
